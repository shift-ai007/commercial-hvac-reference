# Commercial HVAC Emergency Response Guide

What to do when commercial HVAC fails in South Florida — where "no AC" means business closure, product loss, or health risk within hours.

## Severity Classification

| Level | Condition | Response Time | Example |
|-------|-----------|---------------|---------|
| Critical | Total cooling loss in occupied space >85°F | Immediate (< 2 hours) | Server room, medical facility, restaurant during service |
| High | Partial cooling loss, some zones affected | Same day (< 8 hours) | One RTU down in a multi-unit building |
| Medium | Reduced capacity, space at 78-82°F | Next business day | Aging unit struggling on peak-heat day |
| Low | Noise, minor leak, cosmetic issue | Scheduled service | Vibration, minor condensate overflow |

## Immediate Actions by Failure Type

### Total System Failure (No Cooling)
1. **Check the thermostat** — verify it's set to COOL, fan AUTO, temp below current room temp
2. **Check the breaker** — commercial systems have dedicated breakers (often in a separate panel near the unit)
3. **Check the disconnect** — there's usually a weatherproof disconnect box near the outdoor unit. Make sure it's ON
4. **Reset the unit** — turn breaker OFF for 60 seconds, then ON. Wait 5 minutes before the compressor starts
5. **Check the air filter** — a severely clogged filter can trigger a high-pressure safety shutdown
6. If none of these work → call your HVAC contractor for emergency service

### Frozen Evaporator Coil
Signs: warm air from vents, ice on refrigerant lines, water dripping from air handler.
1. Turn the system to FAN ONLY (not off — the fan melts the ice)
2. Check the filter — restricted airflow is the #1 cause of freezing
3. Do NOT scrape ice off the coil — you'll damage the fins
4. Allow 2-4 hours for full defrost
5. If it refreezes after a filter change → likely low refrigerant (call a tech)

### Refrigerant Leak
Signs: gradual loss of cooling over days/weeks, ice on the larger copper line, hissing sound near the unit.
1. Do NOT attempt to add refrigerant yourself (EPA certification required, fines up to $44,539/day)
2. Document the symptoms and timeline for your tech
3. Expect a leak search ($150-400) before repair — adding refrigerant without fixing the leak wastes money

### Water Leak from Unit
Signs: puddles around the air handler, water stains on ceiling tiles, dripping from grilles.
1. Check the condensate drain — clear the trap (pour a cup of vinegar or bleach down the drain line)
2. Check the drain pan — if cracked or rusted, it needs replacement
3. If the leak is from the unit itself, check for ice (frozen coil melting faster than the drain can handle)
4. Place buckets/towels to prevent water damage to inventory, electronics, or flooring

### Unusual Noise
| Sound | Likely Cause | Urgency |
|-------|-------------|---------|
| Screeching/squealing | Belt slipping or failing | High — belt can snap, stopping the blower |
| Banging/clanking | Loose component in blower or compressor | High — turn off to prevent further damage |
| Buzzing | Electrical issue (contactor, relay, capacitor) | Medium — functional but failing |
| Hissing | Refrigerant leak or expansion valve | Medium — losing capacity |
| Clicking (repeated) | Compressor trying to start, failing | High — possible capacitor or compressor failure |

## South Florida-Specific Emergencies

### Post-Hurricane AC Recovery
1. **DO NOT start the AC** until you've inspected the outdoor unit:
   - Remove debris (branches, leaves, standing water)
   - Check that the unit is level on its pad (flooding can shift it)
   - Look for visible damage to refrigerant lines, electrical connections, and fan blades
2. Check the breaker — surge damage may have tripped it
3. If the unit was submerged, do NOT power it on. It needs professional inspection and likely compressor replacement
4. Run the AC on full cool for 2-3 hours to dehumidify — mold starts within 24-48 hours in Florida humidity

### Sustained Power Outage (> 4 Hours)
In South Florida summer, interior temps reach 90°F+ within 3-4 hours of AC loss.
1. Close all blinds and curtains to reduce solar gain
2. Open windows ONLY if outdoor temp is below indoor temp (rare in summer)
3. For temperature-sensitive spaces (server rooms, pharmacies, restaurants): arrange portable cooling immediately — don't wait for power restoration
4. When power returns, wait 5 minutes before starting the AC (compressor protection)

### Flooding
1. If water reached the air handler or ductwork, do not run the system
2. Ductwork contaminated by floodwater must be professionally cleaned or replaced (CDC recommendation)
3. The evaporator coil, blower motor, and control board may need replacement if submerged
4. File an insurance claim immediately — document everything with photos before cleanup

## When to Call a Professional

Always call for:
- Refrigerant issues (EPA-regulated)
- Electrical problems (shock/fire risk)
- Compressor failure (requires specialized tools and recovery equipment)
- Post-flooding inspection
- Any issue you can't diagnose within 30 minutes

## Preventive Measures to Avoid Emergencies

1. **Quarterly preventive maintenance** — not optional in South Florida's climate
2. **Backup portable cooling** — keep a contact for portable AC rental (for server rooms and critical spaces)
3. **Monitoring** — smart thermostats or BMS with alerts when temp exceeds threshold
4. **Spare parts inventory** — keep capacitors, belts, and contactors on-site for common RTU models
5. **Annual duct inspection** — catch leaks and mold before they become emergencies
